# Informal tone vs. Formal tone

<aside>

**Our default approach is professional yet approachable.** We prioritise clarity, empathy, and respect in all communications, but the level of formality varies significantly by market and cultural expectations. Understanding when to use formal or informal language ensures our product feels natural and trustworthy to users in each region.

</aside>

---

## 🏫 General principles

> **Clear and direct**
Avoid jargon and overly complex language.
> 

> **Empathetic**
Acknowledge user needs, concerns, and emotions.
> 

> **Professional**
Maintain credibility, especially in financial contexts.
> 

> **Helpful**
Guide users towards successful outcomes.
> 

> **Respectful**
Honour cultural norms and user preferences.
> 

**The balance between formal and informal shifts based on language and region.**

## 📍Formality by market

### 🇫🇷 — Formal and respectful

**French markets require a consistently formal tone.** Use vouvoiement (vous) and maintain professional distance whilst remaining empathetic and helpful.

**Key rules:**

- **Always use "vous"** (never "tu")
- Use polite formulations: "Veuillez", "Nous vous invitons à", "Merci de"
- Maintain a respectful distance whilst being warm and supportive
- Avoid overly casual expressions or slang

### 🇩🇪 — Flexible based on context

German markets increasingly accept informal tone (Du), particularly in tech and SaaS products, but context matters.

**Key rules:**

- **Default to "Du" (informal)** for most SaaS/tech contexts – it's modern, friendly, and widely accepted
- **Use "Sie" (formal)** only for legal documents (such as T&C)
- Be consistent – don't mix Du and Sie within the same flow
- Even with **Du**, maintain professionalism and clarity

### 🇳🇱 — Informal and friendly

**Dutch markets strongly favour informal tone (je/jij).** Formality (u) feels outdated and distant in SaaS contexts.

**Key rules:**

- **Default to "je/jij" (informal)** for all product communications
- **Use "u" (formal)** only in exceptional legal or compliance contexts, if at all
- Dutch users expect directness and friendliness – overly formal language feels bureaucratic
- Keep language simple, clear, and conversational

### 🇩🇰 — Informal and approachable

**Danish markets overwhelmingly prefer informal tone (du).** Formal address (De) is almost never used in modern contexts.

**Key rules:**

- **Always use "du" (informal)** – formality feels archaic and alienating
- Keep language warm, helpful, and conversational
- Danes value directness and simplicity – avoid corporate jargon
